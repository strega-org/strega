Adaptive Information Processing

Trauma

Most of the time when something difficult happens your brain processes it naturally. It files it away as something in the past. It becomes a memory and not something that you react negatively to. For instance – ‘that was not a nice event but it’s over now and I’m ok about it’.

But

Sometimes if the experience felt overwhelming, happened too quickly or felt very painful the brain struggles to process it and turn it into a memory. Instead it stays unprocessed – almost like it’s frozen in time.

What this can feel like

There’s a head-heart mismatch. Part of you knows something but it doesn’t feel like that.

EMDR

EMDR is based on something called the Adaptive Information Processing (AIP). Your brain has a natural in built system for processing experiences. A bit like how your body is wired to heal from a cut – your brain is wired to heal from painful events.

When everything works well

  • Experiences are processed
  • Linked with other associated memories
  • Stored in helpful ways – ‘It was awful at the time but I learned valuable lessons’.

When something is overwhelming

  • The memory doesn’t fully process
  • You know the event happened in the past but it doesn’t feel that way
  • The thoughts, feelings and body sensations get stuck
  • Reminders make you re-experience those painful thoughts, feelings and bodily sensation – as if you’re reliving it or it is happening all over again.

How EMDR Helps

EMDR helps your brain do what it was meant to do in the first place. During sessions we

  • Gently activate the memory
  • Use eye movements or tapping
  • Allow the brain to reprocess the experience

You don’t have to force anything or figure anything out as the brain will do what it is was built to do naturally.

What changes with EMDR

The memory becomes something you can remember without reliving.

  • The emotional intensity reduces
  • Your body settles
  • Allow the brain to reprocess the experience
  • You are able to link the re-unite both the negative and positive aspects of the event ie: It was truly awful but it’s over, I coped, I survived and I learned something,

You can start responding from the present and with a greater sense of calm and control
